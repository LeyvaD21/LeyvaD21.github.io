Summarizing the Project

This project was a project that not only tested our patience and creativity, but how we approach problems. The initial part of our project was to be hands on making the soldering kit itself, and then soldering it which was a fun throwback form highschool. Once we completed it and began to take measurements, when a lot of do-overs happened with measuring, and double checking we were getting the numbers we wanted. with fusion 360 as our foothold, creating the measurements and adjusting them to make the lightbox first, and then the laser cut box was really interesting and annoying at times. The process took us so long, that we all agreed on another extension during break to actually figure out how we want to design, and create our boxes all while learning the software.

I had the most fun in this project yet, trying to work out the skills that I've developed so far, and working towards sketching, creating, and finally assembling my designs. Using fusion became a lot easier to maneuver, and creating my designs did take a while with trying to get the measurements I wanted, but it did become annoying when trying to extrude and creating adjustments to the lightbox lid. Laser Cutting was a bit more straightforward, and once I was approved for working with the machine, it was so cool to see it work in action. There are definitely a lot of benefits of using laser cutting, and I can hope to work more with it in the future.

I'm happy with the outcome of this project, both with my design among all other designs my classmates created, and having to only do one iteration of the laser cut box, which helped me save so much time. As they say, “measure twice and cut once”, is great advice that I'm listening to alot more. I’ve learned how to better work fusion and make it work for me, and now I'm ready to take on the next big challenge for this course.

Slice and Build

This project began once we were able to open the STL files to download the Unlimited Phoenix hand V3 hand, to which I downloaded the left hand. For this project, we were using IdeaMaker slicer, and imported the parts and also scale them to 150%. Once we did that, I used the Raised3D printer, which has two extrudrs, and was able to have all of the components in the print, besides the Phalanx. After loading the Raise3D with new filament, the print was on its way!

Using the IdeaMaker was new to me, but it was extremely easy to use, and showed a succesful sldie when I imported all of the pieces I wanted. The print took more than a day.

Hand Assembly

My partner and I were able to have all of our compontents printed out by the nxt class, and we accounted for prioritizing the joints and pins. By watching a tutotial video on assembly, and using the e-Nable instructions worksheet, we were able to fully complete the hand. The process itself was really fun and easy, most of it just looking for the right size pin, and fiting it in for fingers. Extremly straightforward and easy, the only difficult part was the stringing of the hands. We had a harder time trying to find the right tension for the hand, but in the end we fine tuned that and got a great outcome on the hand closing.

In the End we ended up completing the hand and it functioned extremly well. Unfortunately I have fat hands so I cannot put my hand all the way in, but im happy that our first hand build was a success!

Remixing the Hand

The remix that I had in mind was to make it easier to brush our pets. The inspiration came for my cat back home, who sheds too much and somehow there is never a brush in sight. I looked into Thingsverse and found this brush that initially had a handle on it, cut it to only have the part I wanted, and fitted it onto the palm of the hand to what I wanted it to be.

Finished Product / Outcome

After coming back from break, the box was complete! To be honest, this was the 3rd iteration that I made, all kept the same but just small adjustments being made along the way. The battery box fit perfectly, sliding in without any problems and plenty of movement to keep it in place. The only place of issue was the light fitting into the rectangles, but that was solved by adjusting the sketch to be closer to the edge. As we see here, the light box fits perfectly into itself, besides some minor extrusion on the ends of the lid, comes off easily and fits perfectly with the kit inside of it.

Sketching on 360

With being tasked to create a 3-D Box, fusion 360 pushed us to a good limit as to where skills are and creating a box. Even when given the resources to work on this box, it still became a hassle when learning the different keys to create and construct the box. While looking at the youtube video and going over how to specifically sketch, it took a lot of effort to learn exactly how many functions fusion360 has. From creating a new component, creating the sketch, and creating the measurements, all the way to extruding it, the whole process took a while, but had a really great learning curve that made it alot better. Some of the benefits of trial and error was getting the hang of creating the sides with the knowledge of creating the base. On the other hand, a huge con was getting the lines to match one another, and trying to flip it to replicate the other side, which created a lot more problems. Overall, I feel like I can create a box from memory, but making sure to get all of the points to match with each other is still a skill I'm working on.

Completed Kit on Fusion360

The laser cut box was a lot more difficult to do, as we needed to learn to use fusion360 to a much bigger extent than how we learned it. I had a lot of versions made for this box, many of them having to deal with specific configurations of construction lines and reading the lines. However, after some trial and error, and following youtube to a very precise amount, we were able to get a completed 3-D box!

Completed Design on Lightburn

After completing the box, I transferred it over to lighburn, a software made for laser cutting and these are the outlines of the box! At first, there were many more lines present, showing all of the extra construction lines I included to make the box in fusion, but then created the outlines by inputting lines into the software, and deleting the lines I didn't need for the print. At the end, it was then to just adjust them to match the cut of the wood, and laser cut my print!

Completed/ Outcome

At the end of the print, I constructed it and had a perfect fit! I still had to use hot glue to make sure it stuck to one another, but once that was completed the whole thing fit perfectly to the model of the kit, and had a great fit in the box. I wished I was a bit more cautious and tried to make it so there was a lid on it for the lights just as the 3-D printed, but besides that I was still happy with it. The Project itself was fun but stressful to play with. The lazarus was straightforward and was able to see how the laser cutter works first hand, but learning the software of lightburn and then trying to make new adjusted lines for it took a while to get, even when a lot of the components that I made got deleted. The assembly itself was easy, and constructing the box in fusion was a bit harder than how I expected it.I enjoyed working on this project, an =d can't wait for a more sophisticated design to work through. This tested a lot of my patience and skills, but the learning curve was great and I feel like I can take on a lot more.
