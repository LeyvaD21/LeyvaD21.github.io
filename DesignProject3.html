Design Project 3

Goals: The overall project goal is to learn to create a 3D Printed, and laser cut enclosure for a light sounds soldering kit. By First learning to solder and compose a kit, we then begin to measure and design the enclosures a 1st iteration of 3-D print, and then a 2nd enclosure for laser cutting. The idea for the enclosure is to have access to 3 features, to be able to see the lights, allow the microphone to hear, and have access for the batteries.

Soldering Kit: Process and Completion

During class we began our kit, this specific kit is a sound sensitive, light changing kit that reacts to noise, and once it captures the noise, it changes colors along the lights. We started by following insutrction from its website, and soldering it during class. The completed kit was great, I knew how to solder before from high school, so it took a little bit of practice to get back into the groove for it. I enjoyed it quite a bit to relearn and try not to make any mistakes. Following the safety procedurres during class, soldering took only 30 minues to completely assemble, and was a great start. Here we can see that it works really well, and the back is not as clean as I'd like it to be, but functional doesn't have to mean pretty!

Measurements and Design Process

The first thing I did was to figure out the measurement by using the caliper. As we see from the picture, I took the Length, Width, and Height of both the component and of the battery box, trying to come up with a way to make them fit together in a box. I also soldered it, and double checked everything to something I was happy with. Once I took measurements, I added .5 to the length in order to consider how tight the print may come out to be, and try to prevent it from being too small and tight. By doing this, I tried to create a 3-D image of how I want my vision on Fusion 360 to look like. My design process was to make sure that both the battery and the lightbox can fit on top of one another comfortably. However, since the Light was longer than the battery box, and the battery had more width, I decided to stack it perpendicular to one another. Instead of trying to come up with a way to make the objects slide into one another comfortably, I tried to make it so I can just stack it into itself and make them fit together into one box. Thus, the design came to have both of them fixed in a box, but include ridges on the inside to have a snug fit for the light and the batteries to fix on top of one another.

Completed Design on Fusion360/ Prusa Slicer

The design here I came up with is a simple fit inside for both components. The battery box goes in first vertically, and is accompanied by the lightbox horizontally on top, which is then secured by the left figure that fits inside the right box. The measurements are within .5 mm of one another, again, to make sure there is enough space for the print to consider the filament and the layering of the PLA. I continued to make sure that there would still be a good fit by moving the object using the move tool on top of the box, to make sure that they fit a flash of one another. On the left, we have the life that once flipped, will fall into the box on the right, and have a semi-secure fit into it. This idea is to allow for easy access to change the box, but to also see the lights from the ridges inside. The photo above shows the most recent iteration, with most adjustments made in consideration of the way the lights will fit into the ridges. Once I input the box into the prusa slicer, there were little adjustments to be made, since it had a great fit in general, and the print process looked great.There were no failures when it came to the finished prusa slicer, and the box had a great fit to it, besides a tiny measurement that can be done for a different iteration. There was no need for a brim on either box, and the box itself looked great.

Finished Product / Outcome

After coming back from break, the box was complete! To be honest, this was the 3rd iteration that I made, all kept the same but just small adjustments being made along the way. The battery box fit perfectly, sliding in without any problems and plenty of movement to keep it in place. The only place of issue was the light fitting into the rectangles, but that was solved by adjusting the sketch to be closer to the edge. As we see here, the light box fits perfectly into itself, besides some minor extrusion on the ends of the lid, comes off easily and fits perfectly with the kit inside of it.

Sketching on 360

With being tasked to create a 3-D Box, fusion 360 pushed us to a good limit as to where skills are and creating a box. Even when given the resources to work on this box, it still became a hassle when learning the different keys to create and construct the box. While looking at the youtube video and going over how to specifically sketch, it took a lot of effort to learn exactly how many functions fusion360 has. From creating a new component, creating the sketch, and creating the measurements, all the way to extruding it, the whole process took a while, but had a really great learning curve that made it alot better. Some of the benefits of trial and error was getting the hang of creating the sides with the knowledge of creating the base. On the other hand, a huge con was getting the lines to match one another, and trying to flip it to replicate the other side, which created a lot more problems. Overall, I feel like I can create a box from memory, but making sure to get all of the points to match with each other is still a skill I'm working on.

Completed Kit on Fusion360

The laser cut box was a lot more difficult to do, as we needed to learn to use fusion360 to a much bigger extent than how we learned it. I had a lot of versions made for this box, many of them having to deal with specific configurations of construction lines and reading the lines. However, after some trial and error, and following youtube to a very precise amount, we were able to get a completed 3-D box!

Completed Design on Lightburn

After completing the box, I transferred it over to lighburn, a software made for laser cutting and these are the outlines of the box! At first, there were many more lines present, showing all of the extra construction lines I included to make the box in fusion, but then created the outlines by inputting lines into the software, and deleting the lines I didn't need for the print. At the end, it was then to just adjust them to match the cut of the wood, and laser cut my print!

Completed/ Outcome

At the end of the print, I constructed it and had a perfect fit! I still had to use hot glue to make sure it stuck to one another, but once that was completed the whole thing fit perfectly to the model of the kit, and had a great fit in the box. I wished I was a bit more cautious and tried to make it so there was a lid on it for the lights just as the 3-D printed, but besides that I was still happy with it. The Project itself was fun but stressful to play with. The lazarus was straightforward and was able to see how the laser cutter works first hand, but learning the software of lightburn and then trying to make new adjusted lines for it took a while to get, even when a lot of the components that I made got deleted. The assembly itself was easy, and constructing the box in fusion was a bit harder than how I expected it.I enjoyed working on this project, an =d can't wait for a more sophisticated design to work through. This tested a lot of my patience and skills, but the learning curve was great and I feel like I can take on a lot more.
